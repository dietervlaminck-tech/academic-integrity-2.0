### Page: "The house rules" (NEW — verified against the two policy PDFs)
> Nyenrode's AI policy rests on **three principles**:
>
> 1. **Academic Integrity** — refrain from fraud in any form and stay open and transparent when working with AI. *Presenting generative-AI output as your own work is fraud.* You may use AI tools to improve readability, language and writing style, but not to replace the core tasks of writing, such as developing scholarly insights or conclusions. You remain responsible for the content.
> 2. **Informed Participation** — have a basic understanding of AI so you can make deliberate decisions about its use: know the model, its training data and biases, how your input is used and stored, and the privacy (AVG), copyright and trusted-environment issues involved. Do not enter personal or confidential data into open models.
> 3. **Accountability** — take full responsibility for every decision to use AI and for the content it generates. Be critical and transparent about all AI use.
>
> **How to disclose:** include your generative-AI use in an appendix or in footnotes on the page where the output is used, specifying the purpose, which parts were generated, and which tool you used (APA provides a citation format for AI tools).
>
> **For your research** (Guidelines responsible use GenAI in research, stoplight approach): language and style editing and integrity checks score *high* on desirability; literature search, figure generation, idea generation and summarisation are *medium/low* and require human validation and manual fact-checking; drafting text and data analysis are *low* and demand extra caution; **AI peer review and AI-generated research data are not permitted** (the only exception: synthetic data as a fully disclosed research methodology). Across all publishers and the European Commission guidelines one rule is universal: **AI can never be an author or co-author**, and the human researcher remains accountable.

## MODULE 2 — THE MACHINE ROOM (prerequisite: Module 1) — NEW

*Learning outcome: explain how a large language model generates text (tokens, context, next-token prediction, reasoning models).*

### Page: "The machine on the desk"
> Up close, the Ghostwriter is disappointingly unmysterious: a box that takes text in and pushes text out. To see through its words you must first understand its trick.
>
> Watch: **Large Language Models explained briefly** (3Blue1Brown, 8 min): https://youtu.be/LPZh9BOjkQs
>
> Remember one sentence: *a language model does not know things; it predicts the most probable next token given the context it was given.*

### Page: "Think like a transformer"
> Try the machine's job yourself.
>
> **Dieter eet graag…?** — a tosti? a gazon? You know it is not "gazon". Why? Context.
> **Dieter houdt van tomaten en kaas. Hij lust geen brood. Hij eet graag…?** — now "salade Caprese" beats "tosti". More context, better prediction.
>
> This is everything the Ghostwriter does, billions of times per answer. The more context you give it, the more precise its prediction — but there is always an element of chance. That is why the same question can produce different answers, and why an answer can be fluent and wrong at the same time.
>
> *Ork, ork, ork, soep eet je met een…?* A pure language-pattern machine says **vork** (it rhymes!). A reasoning model pauses: "You don't eat soup with a fork. This is a joke — but the sensible answer is **lepel**."
>
> Watch: **This Is How Reasoning LLMs Really Work** (https://youtu.be/ygRDcMWHDy0) to see what changed in 2025 — and what did not.

### Quiz: "The control panel" (graded, min. 8/10 to continue)
1. (MC) What does a large language model fundamentally do? — **Predict the most probable next token given a context** / Look up answers in a database / Copy sentences from the internet / Reason logically about facts
2. (Multiple answers) Which factors make an LLM's output more precise? — **More specific context in the prompt** · **Clear task and role description** / Asking it to be honest / Typing in capital letters
3. (MC) Why can an LLM produce a fluent but false answer? — **Because it optimises for probable language, not for truth** / Because it is programmed to lie / Because it always lacks data
4. (MC) What distinguishes a reasoning model from a classic LLM? — **It generates intermediate reasoning steps before answering** / It has feelings / It is always correct / It refuses jokes
5. (Essay, ungraded) Explain in two sentences, to a colleague, why "the AI said so" is not a source.

### Quiz gate: "The maintenance hatch" — access code `TOKEN`
> Behind the control panel you find the machine's fuel etched into a plate: TOKEN. You also spot a second key, shaped like the letter **C**. Take it with you and continue to the desk.

## MODULE 3 — THE STUDY (prerequisite: Module 2)

*Learning outcomes: evaluate AI output (verify sources, spot fabrication); conduct an integrity conversation.*

### Page: "The desk"
> The antique desk of Albert Plesman, 1946. His forgotten notebook is still here — but the pile of paperwork on top is new, and suspiciously well-written.

### Quiz: "The notebook" (1 pt + survey)
> Plesman's ponderings: *How do we know what is real and what is fake, in a world suddenly dominated by generative AI?*
1. (Multiple answers, graded) You want to determine whether a paper may have been generated by AI. Which aspects might help? — **Style of language differs from the student's** · **Sources may be fabricated or refer to nonexistent works** · **Absurd or irrational claims** / "AI always makes untrue claims" (wrong: *always* is the giveaway)
2. (MC, graded) A reference list contains "Springer. (n.d.). Global Sustainability (pp. 3-17)". What is the strongest reason for suspicion? — **A publisher is listed as an author with no date and no traceable work** / It is not alphabetised / Page numbers are included / It cites a book
3. (MC, graded) According to Nyenrode's AI policy, which use of generative AI in a written assignment is permitted *without* it counting as fraud? — **Improving readability, language and writing style, with disclosure** / Generating the conclusions / Writing the analysis section / Any use, as long as the text is paraphrased
4. (MC, graded) Where does the policy say AI use must be disclosed? — **In an appendix or in footnotes on the page where the output is used, naming purpose, parts and tool** / In a private note to the examiner / Nowhere, disclosure is voluntary / Only when more than half the text is generated
5. (Essay, survey) What is your personal first check when you suspect AI-generated work?

## MODULE 4 — THE EXAMINER'S WORKSHOP (prerequisite: Module 3) — merges Cursus Toetsing en Examinering

*Learning outcome: design (or redesign) an assessment that remains valid in an AI-rich environment, using constructive alignment, a toetsmatrijs and the four quality criteria.*

### Page: "The chest of drawers"
> The chest of drawers turns out to be an examiner's cabinet: every drawer a tool. Generative AI has not broken assessment; it has exposed assessment that was already weak. Tasks that ask for reproduction of available knowledge were poor measures before the Ghostwriter could do them in seconds. The answer is not surveillance but design.

### Page: "Drawer 1 — Constructive alignment"
> Leerdoelen, leeractiviteiten en toetsing in één lijn (Biggs). Name the outcome as a verb-led performance; show the activity in which learners practise exactly that performance; show the assessment that checks it. If any of the three names a different performance, the design is misaligned — and misaligned designs are exactly where AI shortcuts thrive.
> [Reuse the *Constructive alignment*, *Taxonomie van Bloom* and *Toetsmatrijs* pages from Cursus Toetsing en Examinering.]

### Page: "Drawer 2 — The four quality criteria"
> Validiteit, betrouwbaarheid, transparantie, bruikbaarheid. An AI-resilient assessment that fails any of these is not yet fit for use. Note the governance route: changing an assessment form runs through the Alternative Assessment procedure (Assessment Committee advises, Exam Committee approves) — never a unilateral act.
> [Reuse *Kwaliteitscriteria toetsing*, *Toetscyclus*, *Rubrics* pages.]

### Page: "Drawer 3 — Three moves that keep assessment valid"
> 1. **Assess process as well as product** (drafts, reflections, oral defence).
> 2. **Make it authentic** (messy, contextual problems that generic AI answers fail).
> 3. **Weight assessment for learning** (frequent low-stakes checks; feedback and feedforward).
> Where the outcome *includes* skilled AI use, require and examine that use: let students critique, correct and improve AI output rather than pretending the tool does not exist.

### Assignment: "Redesign one of your own assessments" (submission required)
> Take one assessment from your own module. In max. 1 A4:
> 1. State the learning outcome (verb-led, Bloom level).
> 2. Diagnose: where could the Ghostwriter currently do the work the outcome requires of the learner?
> 3. Redesign: apply at least one of the three moves, and show the alignment chain (outcome → activity → assessment).
> 4. Disclose: state where you used AI in producing this redesign, and how you verified its suggestions.
> *Optional but encouraged: use a TRACI-structured prompt (Task, Role, Audience, Constraints/Create, Intent) to co-design with an AI, then critique its output in your submission.*

### Discussion: "Peer review corner" (contribute)
> Post one insight from your redesign, and respond to one colleague: does their alignment chain hold? Would their assessment survive a student with unlimited AI access?
